Dynamics

Momentum, impulse, and collisions

Momentum is one of the most fundamental concepts in dynamical systems. It is often convenient to derive equations of motion using momentum. Furthermore, it is related to kinetic energy and Newton's second law. Conservation of momentum is one of the three conservation laws in dynamics alongside conservation of energy and mass.

Linear momentum of a rigid body.

\[ \vec{p} = m\vec{v}_C \]
where $\vec{v}_C$ is the velocity of the center of mass.

Unlike energy, momentum is a vector, and its direction is the same as the direction of the velocity of the center of mass.

Furthermore, angular momentum is analogous to linear momentum. The angular momentum of a rigid body is shown below.

Angular momentum of a rigid body rotating about principal axis $\hat{a}$ through point $P$.

\[ \vec{H}_P = I_{P,\, \hat{a}} \vec{\omega} \]
where $I_{P,\, \hat{a}}$ is the moment of inertia about axis $\hat{a}$ through point $P$.

Momentum is also closely linked to force and moments. More specifically, the change in momentum and Newton's second law can be related.

Newton's second law and linear momentum.

\[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}\\ &= \dot{\vec{p}} \end{aligned}\]
where $\sum \vec{F}_{ext}$ is the sum of the external forces acting on the body.

We begin with Newton's second law: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= m\vec{a}_C\\ &= m \frac{d\vec{v}_C}{dt} \\ \end{aligned}\]

Assuming constant mass, then this can be rewritten as: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= \frac{d}{dt} (m\vec{v}_C)\\ & = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} \\ & = \dot{\vec{p}} \end{aligned}\]

As aforementioned, angular momentum is analogous to linear momentum, so the above equation can also be derived using moments and angular momentum.

Newton's second law and angular momentum.

\[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt}\\ &= \dot{\vec{H}} \end{aligned}\]
where $\sum \vec{M}_{ext}$ is the sum of the external moments acting on the body.

We begin with Newton's second law: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= I_{C, \hat{a}}\vec{\alpha}\\ &= I_{C, \hat{a}} \frac{d\vec{\omega}}{dt} \\ \end{aligned}\]

Assuming constant moment of inertia, then this can be rewritten as: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= \frac{d}{dt} (I_{C, \hat{a}}\vec{\omega})\\ & = \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt} \\ & = \dot{\vec{H}} \end{aligned}\]

From the above equations, it is clear to see that linear momentum is conserved if and only if the sum of external forces acting on the body is zero, and angular momentum is conserved if and only if the sum of external moments acting on the body is zero.

You may have seen other notations for angular momentum (e.g $\vec{L}$). The common notation is $\vec{H}$, and that is what will be used in this class.
