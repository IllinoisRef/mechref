Dynamics

Momentum, impulse, and collisions

Momentum is one of the most fundamental concepts in dynamical systems. It is often convenient to derive equations of motion using momentum. Furthermore, it is related to kinetic energy and Newton's second law. Conservation of momentum is one of the three conservation laws in dynamics alongside conservation of energy and mass.

Linear momentum of a rigid body.

\[ \vec{p} = m\vec{v}_C \]
where $\vec{v}_C$ is the velocity of the center of mass.

Unlike energy, momentum is a vector, and its direction is the same as the direction of the velocity of the center of mass.

Furthermore, angular momentum is analogous to linear momentum. The angular momentum of a rigid body is shown below.

Angular momentum of a rigid body rotating about principal axis $\hat{a}$ through point $P$.

\[ \vec{H}_P = I_{P,\, \hat{a}} \vec{\omega} \]
where $I_{P,\, \hat{a}}$ is the moment of inertia about axis $\hat{a}$ through point $P$.

Momentum is also closely linked to force and moments. More specifically, the change in momentum and Newton's second law can be related.

Newton's second law and linear momentum.

\[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}\\ &= \dot{\vec{p}} \end{aligned}\]
where $\sum \vec{F}_{ext}$ is the sum of the external forces acting on the body.

We begin with Newton's second law: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= m\vec{a}_C\\ &= m \frac{d\vec{v}_C}{dt} \\ \end{aligned}\]

Assuming constant mass, then this can be rewritten as: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= \frac{d}{dt} (m\vec{v}_C)\\ & = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} \\ & = \dot{\vec{p}} \end{aligned}\]

Warning: $\sum \vec{F}_{ext} = \dot{\vec{p}}$ is not applicable with variable mass.

In the derivation of #ren-mn, the mass was assumed to be constant. In the case of a rocket expelling propellant, this does not work. See the rocket equation derivation further down.

As aforementioned, angular momentum is analogous to linear momentum, so the above equation can also be derived using moments and angular momentum.

Newton's second law and angular momentum.

\[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt}\\ &= \dot{\vec{H}} \end{aligned}\]
where $\sum \vec{M}_{ext}$ is the sum of the external moments acting on the body.

We begin with Newton's second law: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= I_{C, \hat{a}}\vec{\alpha}\\ &= I_{C, \hat{a}} \frac{d\vec{\omega}}{dt} \\ \end{aligned}\]

Assuming constant moment of inertia, then this can be rewritten as: \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= \frac{d}{dt} (I_{C, \hat{a}}\vec{\omega})\\ & = \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt} \\ & = \dot{\vec{H}} \end{aligned}\]

From the above equations, it is clear to see that linear momentum is conserved if and only if the sum of external forces acting on the system is zero, and angular momentum is conserved if and only if the sum of external moments acting on the system is zero.

Conservation of linear momentum.

\[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{F}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}\\ &= \vec{0} \end{aligned}\]

Conservation of angular momentum.

\[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt}\\ &= \vec{0} \end{aligned}\]

Interestingly, angular momentum is also related to linear momentum if the object is a point mass.

Angular momentum of a point mass $P$ about a stationary point $O$.

\[ \vec{H}_{O} = \vec{r}_{OP} \times \vec{p}_P \]

Begin with the relationship between angular momentum and moment at #rec-mm \[\begin{aligned} \sum \vec{M}_{ext} &= \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt}\\ \end{aligned}\]

For simplicity's sake, assume there's only one external moment due to a force applied at point $P$. Recall the definition of moment due to a force applied at point $P$: \[\begin{aligned} \vec{M}_O &= \vec{r}_{OP} \times \vec{F}_P \\ \end{aligned}\]

Then #rec-mm becomes: \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt} &= \vec{r}_{OP} \times \vec{F}_P \end{aligned}\]

Using the relationship between linear momentum and force at #rec-mn , the above equation becomes: \[\begin{aligned} \frac{d\vec{H}}{dt} &= \vec{r}_{OP} \times \vec{F}_P \\ & = \vec{r}_{OP} \times \frac{d\vec{p}_P}{dt} \end{aligned}\]

Multiplying both sides by $dt$ yields: \[ d\vec{H} = \vec{r}_{OP} \times d\vec{p}_P \]

Recall integration by parts: \[ \int u \, dw = uw - \int w \, du \]

Setting $dw = d\vec{p}_P$ and $u = \vec{r}_{OP}$, then: \[\begin{aligned} u = \vec{r}_{OP} \quad \quad \quad du = \vec{v}_P \,dt \\ w = \vec{p}_P \quad \quad \quad dw = d\vec{p}_P \end{aligned}\]

Therefore: \[\begin{aligned} \int d\vec{H} &= \int \vec{r}_{OP} \times d\vec{p}_P \\ &= \vec{r}_{OP} \times \vec{p}_P - \int \vec{p}_P \times \vec{v}_P \,dt \end{aligned}\]

However, $\vec{p}_P = m\vec{v}_P$ as established in #rec-lm: \[\begin{aligned} \vec{H} &= \vec{r}_{OP} \times \vec{p}_P - \int m\vec{v}_P \times \vec{v}_P \,dt \end{aligned}\]

The cross product of a vector with itself is zero (cross product self-annihilation). So the integral of $\int m\vec{v}_P \times \vec{v}_P \, dt$ is a constant, which is the initial angular momentum. Assuming this is zero, we arrive at the desired expression.

You may have seen other notations for angular momentum (e.g $\vec{L}$). The common notation is $\vec{H}$, and that is what will be used in this class.

Impulse

Impulse is a quantity that describes the effect of a resultant force acting over time. Naturally, impulse is related to momentum.

Impulse.

\[ \vec{J} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \vec{F}_{net} \, dt \]

Note that impulse, like momentum, is a vector. Its direction is the same direction as the net force.

As seen from the equation above, this naturally leads to relating the impulse and momentum. Specifically, impulse is the change in momentum.

Impulse-momentum theorem.

\[ \vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p} \]

From Newton's second law and linear momentum at #rec-mn: \[ \vec{F}_{net} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} \]

Plugging into the impulse definition: \[\begin{aligned} \vec{J} &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} \, dt \\ &= \int_{\vec{p}_1}^{\vec{p}_2} d\vec{p} \\ &= \vec{p}_2 - \vec{p}_1 = \Delta \vec{p} \end{aligned}\]
